Most resume problems are not about experience. They are about presentation.

The experience is there. The accomplishments are real. But somewhere between what you did and what the page says, something got lost. The language is vague. The bullet points describe duties instead of results. The document reads like a job description instead of a case for why you are the right person.

Here is what tends to go wrong most often.

**Weak openings.** Most resumes open with a summary that says nothing specific. "Results-driven professional with over five years of experience" could describe anyone. Start with what makes you distinct.

**Passive bullet points.** "Responsible for managing the team" is weaker than "Managed a team of eight across three concurrent projects." The second version shows the work. The first just names the category.

**No sense of scale.** Numbers matter. How many? How much? How often? Even approximate figures give the reader something to hold on to.

**Generic language.** If your resume could belong to someone else in your field, it needs more of you in it. The language should reflect how you actually work, not how resumes are supposed to sound.
