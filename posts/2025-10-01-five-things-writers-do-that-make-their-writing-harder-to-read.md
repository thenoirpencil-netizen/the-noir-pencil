These are not mistakes, exactly. They are habits. And most writers do not notice them until someone points them out.

### 1. Burying the main point.

Most writers open with context and build toward their main idea. Most readers want the main idea first. Lead with what matters. Let the context support it.

### 2. Writing sentences that do too much.

One idea per sentence is usually enough. When a sentence is carrying three ideas at once, it is working too hard and asking the reader to work too hard with it.

### 3. Using weak verbs.

"Is," "was," "has," and "were" are not bad words. But they are often placeholders for a stronger verb that would do more work. "The report was written by the team" becomes "The team wrote the report." Shorter, cleaner, more direct.

### 4. Repeating words in close proximity.

When the same word appears twice in three sentences, the reader notices. It does not always need to be fixed, but it is worth a look every time.

### 5. Hedging when you do not need to.

"I think," "it seems like," "in some ways," "kind of." These phrases are natural in speech. In writing, they can soften an idea past the point of usefulness. If you believe something, say it directly.

None of these are fatal. They are just patterns worth watching.
