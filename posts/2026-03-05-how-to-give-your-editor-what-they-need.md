The editing relationship works best when it goes both ways. Your editor brings expertise. You bring context. The more clearly you can communicate what the document is for and what you need from the edit, the better the result.

Before you send your document, it helps to be able to answer a few things. Who is this for? What is it supposed to do? Is there a specific deadline? Are there parts you feel uncertain about? Is there anything you want the editor to leave alone?

That last one matters more than most writers realize. If there is a section you feel strongly about, say so. A good editor will still flag it if something is not working, but they will do it with the context that you have a reason for it.

It also helps to be honest about where the document is. If you know the structure is rough, say that. If you think the grammar is mostly clean but the tone is off, say that too. The more specific you can be, the more targeted the edit will be.

You do not have to know what is wrong with the document. That is the editor's job. But the more you can share about what you were trying to do, the easier it is to help you do it.
